## 1. Portfolio Positioning

### Your Title
Use **"Visual Designer"** or **"Graphic Designer & Art Director"**.

"Visual Designer" bridges the gap beautifully — it implies you handle everything from digital interfaces to brand aesthetics. It does not box you into either fashion or graphic design.

**Never use "Fashion & Graphic Designer"** — it dilutes your expertise to a hiring manager scanning hundreds of CVs. It reads as indecisive.

### The Fashion Edge — Your Superpower
Your fashion background is not a weakness. Reframe it:
- Fashion taught you **visual storytelling** — how to build a narrative across a collection
- Fashion taught you **art direction** — directing photoshoots, curating aesthetics, controlling a mood
- Fashion taught you **colour theory at an advanced level** — not just picking hex codes, but understanding how colour behaves on material, in light, and in context
- Fashion taught you **silhouette and proportion** — which is directly transferable to layout and composition

**The pitch to a graphic design agency:** "I have the technical skills of a graphic designer and the editorial eye of a fashion art director."

### Avoiding the "Fashion Student" Trap
- Remove photos of sewing machines, fabric swatches, or runway backstage snapshots unless they are explicitly part of a branded campaign case study
- Never lead with garment construction — lead with brand identity and visual communication
- Your fashion work must be **repackaged as brand identity or editorial work**
- Any fashion project on your portfolio must have a graphic design component attached to it (lookbook layout, campaign posters, brand identity, social media)

### Balancing Fashion + Graphic Design
Structure your portfolio into clear categories so employers know exactly what they are looking at:

| Category | Label to Use | What Goes Here |
|----------|-------------|----------------|
| Graphic Design | "Branding & Identity" | Logo systems, brand guidelines, packaging |
| Graphic Design | "Editorial & Layout" | Lookbooks, magazines, posters, reports |
| Graphic Design | "Digital & Social" | App UI, social media toolkits, email campaigns |
| Fashion | "Apparel & Technical" | Tech packs, flats, line sheets, collections |

## 2. Portfolio Website Structure

### The 30-Second Rule
Recruiters spend **15 to 30 seconds** on a portfolio before deciding to stay or leave.

Above the fold (before any scrolling) they must see:
- Your name
- Your title
- High-quality thumbnail images of your **best 3 projects**

No long introductions. No loading screens. No password gates on your main work. **Show, do not tell.**

### Navigation — Keep It Minimal
Three pages maximum:
1. **Work** (Homepage — your project index)
2. **About** (Your story, portrait photo, CV download link)
3. **Contact** (Email, LinkedIn, Instagram — one clean page)

### Platform Comparison

| Platform | Best For | Pros | Cons |
|----------|---------|------|------|
| **Framer** | Modern, tech-forward designers | Incredible animations, industry-standard feel | Steeper learning curve |
| **Webflow** | Highly customised agency aesthetics | Ultimate control, great for UI/UX credibility | Requires basic HTML/CSS logic |
| **Squarespace** | Editorial, clean, image-heavy | Beautiful templates, fast to build | Can look generic |
| **Wix Studio** | Advanced visual layouts | Great drag-and-drop, solid animation tools | Can feel bloated if overdesigned |

**Recommendation:** Use **Squarespace** to launch fast, or **Framer** if you want to show you understand modern web design conventions. Since your website already runs on Next.js with Framer Motion, your custom-built site is better than any platform — it already signals technical and design competence.

### Case Study Structure — What Every Project Page Must Contain
Every project page on your site should follow this exact flow:

```
1. PROJECT TITLE — Large, typographic, full-width
2. META — Year | Type (Branding / Editorial / Fashion) | Status (Concept / Live)
3. BRIEF — 1–3 sentences describing the problem or prompt
4. CHALLENGE — What made this difficult or interesting
5. SOLUTION — Your design approach and rationale
6. PROCESS — Sketches, moodboards, iterations, rejected ideas
7. COLOUR PALETTE — Swatches with names or hex codes
8. TYPOGRAPHY — Fonts used with specimen examples
9. FINAL OUTCOMES — Best quality mockups and images (full width)
10. MOCKUPS — Real-world application images (on walls, phones, clothing, packaging)
```

## 3. Graphic Design Portfolio

### What Hiring Managers Need to See

For a **graphic design agency** role:
- Brand identity system (not just a logo — the full language)
- Editorial layout (shows you understand grids and typography)
- Social media design (this is the bread-and-butter junior task)
- At least one digital product or UI screen

For a **corporate in-house** role:
- Ability to make information look beautiful (reports, decks, data)
- Consistency across formats
- Clean, professional typography — no decorative fonts where they do not belong

For **freelance**:
- Range — show you can work across categories
- Mockup quality — clients buy the vision, not the flat file

### What Separates a Beginner from a Hireable Portfolio

| Beginner | Hireable |
|---------|---------|
| Inconsistent work — one great project, four weak ones | Every project is equally strong |
| Logos presented flat on white backgrounds | Every logo shown on premium, realistic mockups |
| Random font combinations | Intentional, documented type systems |
| No case study context — just images | Brief, challenge, solution, process shown |
| Generic Canva-style layouts | Custom, considered grid structures |
| Typefaces stretched or distorted | Correct kerning, no distortion |
| Widows and orphans in text | Clean typesetting throughout |

## 4. Fashion Portfolio

### How to Include Fashion Without Weakening Graphic Design Positioning

Create a dedicated **"Apparel & Technical"** category. Do not mix garment construction images with brand identity work on the same page.

**The Rule:** Every fashion project must have a graphic design component. A standalone tech pack is valid — but a tech pack alongside the brand identity for that garment's label is twice as strong.

### How to Present Fashion Work Digitally
- **Lookbooks** — lay out editorial photography in InDesign, export as PDF or interactive web page
- **Campaign posters** — take fashion photography and apply strong typographic treatment
- **Brand identity** — design the logo, labels, hangtags, and packaging for the brand you are designing for
- **Social media campaigns** — design the Instagram launch content for a collection

### Connecting Fashion to Graphic Design Skills
| Fashion Element | Graphic Design Translation |
|----------------|--------------------------|
| Collection moodboard | Art direction / visual research |
| Lookbook photography | Editorial layout and typography |
| Technical flat sketches | Vector illustration |
| Garment labels and hangtags | Print design and packaging |
| Collection campaign | Brand identity and advertising |
| Trend forecasting deck | Presentation design and data layout |

## 5. Skills Roadmap

### Month 1 — Vectors (Adobe Illustrator)
**Focus:** Logo construction, typography manipulation, vector flat sketching

Master these tools:
- Pen tool (bezier curves — non-negotiable)
- Pathfinder panel (combining and subtracting shapes)
- Typography: kerning, tracking, converting text to outlines
- Artboard management for multi-format exports

**Practice projects:** Logo explorations, menswear technical flat sketches, typographic poster layouts

---

### Month 2 — Pixels (Adobe Photoshop)
**Focus:** Masking, colour grading, Smart Objects, mockup application

Master these tools:
- Layer masks and clipping masks
- Camera Raw and colour grading
- Smart Objects — how to drop your designs into photorealistic mockups
- Non-destructive editing (adjustment layers only — never edit the base layer)

**Practice projects:** Apply your Month 1 logos to mockups, colour-grade editorial photography, create composite campaign images

---

### Month 3 — Layouts (Adobe InDesign)
**Focus:** Master pages, paragraph styles, grid systems, print-ready documents

Master these tools:
- Master pages and parent pages
- Paragraph and character styles (never format text manually)
- Baseline grid alignment
- Bleed, slug, and print marks for print-ready PDFs

**Practice projects:** Digital lookbook layout, menswear line sheet, trend forecasting deck

---

### Month 4 — Digital (Figma)
**Focus:** Auto-layout, components, prototyping, digital grids

Master these tools:
- Auto-layout (essential for UI design)
- Component system (like a design system — buttons, cards, nav bars)
- Basic prototyping (linking screens together)
- Design tokens and styles

**Practice projects:** E-commerce app screens for a menswear brand, social media template system

---

### Month 5 — Concept Creation
**Focus:** Generating and executing fictional briefs at a professional standard

Tasks:
- Write briefs for your 4–6 portfolio projects
- Execute all design work using skills from Months 1–4
- Build case studies around the work
- Source premium mockups (Mockup Maison, Artboard Studio, Unblast)
- Photograph or compile imagery for lookbook layouts

---

### Month 6 — Portfolio Build & Launch
**Focus:** Curating, presenting, applying

Tasks:
- Select your best 4–6 projects only — cut everything else
- Build your website (or update your existing Next.js site)
- Format and export your portfolio PDF (landscape, under 10MB)
- Design your CV using your personal brand typography
- Begin outreach on LinkedIn and Bizcommunity

## 6. Making It Competitive

### Hiring Manager Secrets

**Consistency is King**
I judge you on your worst project. If five projects are excellent and one is weak, the weak one raises doubts. Only show your absolute best, even if that means showing only three projects.

**Typography is the Tell**
I can spot a junior designer in seconds by how they handle type:
- Are the letter-spacings appropriate for the size? (Tight for headlines, normal for body)
- Are there widows or orphans? (Single words stranded on a line)
- Are fonts stretched or distorted? (Instant rejection)
- Is the type hierarchy clear? (Can I read the page in the right order without effort?)

**Mockup Quality is Everything**
A mediocre logo on a premium mockup looks like professional work. A great logo on a white background looks like a student exercise. Use mockup sites like:
- Mockup Maison
- Artboard Studio
- Unblast
- Smartmockups

Never use low-resolution, watermarked, or obviously generic mockups.

**The South African Market Specifically**
Agencies in Johannesburg and Cape Town value hybrid designers who can:
- Do branding (logos, systems, guidelines)
- Create social media content (the everyday task)
- Touch basic motion graphics (After Effects is a bonus skill)
- Handle a camera or direct photography

If you can do all four, you are in the top 10% of applicants for most junior roles in SA.

### Common Mistakes That Cause Instant Rejection
- Portfolio password-protected with no way to see the work in advance
- More than 6 projects — dilution looks like insecurity
- Inconsistent personal brand (CV looks nothing like the portfolio)
- No case study context — just dumping images with no explanation
- Showing every project you have ever done instead of only the best
- Garment construction photos without a design context

## 7. Getting Hired and Freelancing

### Where to Find Jobs (South Africa)
- **Bizcommunity** — the primary source for SA creative industry jobs
- **LinkedIn** — essential for corporate in-house and remote roles
- **Careers24** — broader reach, includes creative agency listings
- **PNet** — corporate design roles
- **Instagram** — follow agencies and studios, many post openings on their feeds
- Direct agency outreach — find studios you admire and email your portfolio unsolicited

### Where to Find Remote Jobs
- **Contra** — freelance platform popular with designers globally
- **Toptal** — higher barrier to entry, but much higher rates
- **Behance Job Board** — design-specific listings globally
- **LinkedIn Remote Filter** — filter any design search by "Remote"
- **We Work Remotely** — tech-adjacent design roles

### Your CV
- One page, PDF only
- **Design your own CV** — do not use a Canva template with skill progress bars
- Use the same typography and colour palette from your personal brand
- Sections: Summary (2 sentences), Skills, Experience, Education, Software
- Link to your portfolio and LinkedIn in the header
- When a hiring manager sees your CV, portfolio, and LinkedIn all using the same visual language, they see a professional — not a student

### Freelancing — Getting Your First Clients
**Before you have experience:**
- Approach friends, family, and local small businesses
- Offer a rebrand at a low cost in exchange for full creative control and the right to use the work in your portfolio
- Ask every person you know if they know someone who needs a logo, a social media package, or a rebrand

**Pricing (ZAR — Entry Level)**
- Hourly rate: R250–R350/hour (junior freelance standard in SA)
- Logo + brand identity (small startup): R3,500–R6,000
- Social media monthly package (12 posts + stories): R2,500–R4,500/month
- Lookbook or editorial layout (12–15 pages): R3,000–R5,500
- Tech pack (single garment): R1,500–R3,000

**Project-based pricing is always better than hourly.** Clients feel comfortable knowing the total upfront, and you are rewarded for working efficiently.

## 10. Step-by-Step Project Execution Guide

### The Master Project — One Brand, All Deliverables
The most efficient approach is to build one fictional menswear brand and produce all deliverables from it. This shows you can think like a Creative Director who understands a brand's entire ecosystem.

---

### Phase 1 — Ideation and Concept (The Seed)

**Step 1: Choose your menswear niche**
Pick one specific category. Do not try to straddle multiple niches:
- Technical / Utilitarian (think Acronym, Arc'teryx Veilance)
- Modern Tailoring (think Brioni, Thom Sweeney)
- Streetwear (think Stüssy, Fear of God, Aime Leon Dore)
- Workwear Heritage (think Carhartt WIP, Engineered Garments)
- Minimalist Luxury (think Lemaire, A.P.C., Margaret Howell)

**Step 2: Name and narrative**
Write a 3-sentence brand mission before opening any software:
- Why does this brand exist?
- Who wears it?
- What does it stand for that no one else does?

*Example: "ARKIV creates modular garments for the modern creative professional who needs high-performance gear that does not look like hiking clothes. The brand is built around the idea that clothing should adapt — to weather, to context, to the day's demands. Every garment is designed once and worn indefinitely."*

**Step 3: Visual research (the moodboard)**
Go to Are.na, Behance, Pinterest, and Dezeen.
- Collect 20 images of typography (look for Swiss design, brutalist layouts, or editorial fashion typography)
- Collect 20 images of menswear silhouettes in your chosen niche
- Collect 10 images of material and texture references
- Collect 10 images of brand touchpoints (packaging, labels, storefronts, campaigns)

Do not start designing until you have collected all 60 images and can see a clear visual direction emerging.

---

### Phase 2 — Brand Identity (The Graphic Design Foundation)

**Step 1: Logo construction (Illustrator)**
Design in this order:
1. A primary logo (wordmark or logotype)
2. A secondary mark or icon (something that works small — on a label, on a zip-pull)
3. A monogram or condensed version for tight applications

Avoid: gradients, drop shadows, raster effects, overly decorative letterforms. Aim for something that could sit on a high-end product tag and still feel right in 20 years.

**Step 2: Typography system**
Select exactly two fonts:
- A **heading font** with character (a geometric sans-serif, a modern serif, or something expressive)
- A **body font** that is highly readable at small sizes (Inter, Helvetica Now, Söhne, or similar)

Document: font name, weights used, minimum size, and letter-spacing rules. This becomes your brand guidelines.

**Step 3: Colour palette**
Choose 3–4 colours maximum and assign each a function:
- Primary colour (dominant — used on logos, headings, backgrounds)
- Accent colour (used sparingly — calls to action, highlights)
- Neutral (off-white, stone, concrete — used for large areas)
- Optional: a fourth colour for digital-only use

Avoid using more than 4. Restraint in colour is a sign of sophistication.

**Step 4: Brand graphic language**
Design a visual element that makes the brand immediately recognisable beyond the logo:
- A custom grid or frame system
- A specific way of cropping and positioning photography
- A repeating pattern (for packaging interiors, tissue paper, linings)
- A set of custom icons or graphic marks

---

### Phase 3 — Fashion Technicals (The Designer Proof)

**Step 1: Design the capsule collection**
Choose 5 key menswear pieces aligned with your niche. For a technical brand:
- 1 technical jacket (the hero piece)
- 1 hoodie or sweatshirt
- 1 trousers (cargo, pleated, or straight)
- 1 shirt or overshirt
- 1 accessory (bag, cap, or belt)

**Step 2: Vector flat sketches (Illustrator)**
Draw technical flats for all 5 garments. Rules:
- Black outlines only — no colour fills at this stage
- Consistent line weights: 1pt for outlines, 0.5pt for internal seams and stitching
- Front AND back views for every garment
- Add "callout circles" — zoomed details of construction points (zip type, pocket construction, collar stand, hem finish)
- Present all flats on a neutral grey background at consistent scale

**Step 3: Comprehensive tech pack (the hero garment)**
Choose the most complex garment (usually the jacket) and build a 5-page PDF:
- Page 1: Technical sketch (front, back, and detail callouts)
- Page 2: Bill of Materials (BOM) — every fabric, lining, interfacing, thread, zip, button, and label listed with weight, composition, and colour reference
- Page 3: Measurement table with grading across 3–4 sizes
- Page 4: Construction details (stitch type, seam allowances, pressing instructions)
- Page 5: Brand placement diagram (label placement, care label location, hangtag attachment point)

**Step 4: Line sheet**
Create a 1–2 page B2B document for the full 5-piece collection:
- Small flat sketches of all garments in a clean grid
- Product name, SKU, available colorways (swatches)
- Wholesale price and recommended retail price
- Brand header using your logo and typography system

---

### Phase 4 — Editorial and Layout (The Art Direction Proof)

**Step 1: Source or create photography**
Options, in order of preference:
1. Photograph your own garments (if you have made physical samples) — even a mannequin against a plain wall is better than nothing
2. Style a friend in clothes that match your brand's aesthetic and photograph them — this does not need to be expensive
3. Use high-quality, free editorial photography from Unsplash or Pexels (filter for menswear, editorial, lifestyle)

**Step 2: Digital lookbook (InDesign — 12–15 pages)**
Page-by-page structure:
- **Cover:** One hero image full bleed, brand name large, season/year in small caps
- **Contents:** A grid showing each look with a page number — use your brand grid system
- **Editorial spreads (3–4 double pages):** Vary the pacing — alternate between full-bleed image pages and white space pages with small images and body copy
- **Technical spread:** One page showing a flat sketch alongside its image — bridges the lookbook to your technical work
- **Closing page:** Brand mark, website, and contact

Typography rules for the lookbook:
- Headlines: large, confident, minimal — let the images breathe
- Body copy: small, precise, well-spaced
- Captions: lightest weight available, minimum size
- Never use more than 2 typefaces in the layout

**Step 3: Campaign posters (3 street posters)**
Design 3 "wild posting" or billboard-style posters using your lookbook photography:
- Overlay bold typographic treatments (season name, brand name, a single stark phrase)
- Add small "graphic noise" — coordinates, technical specifications, material codes — to make the posters feel detailed and considered
- Present them on a wall mockup (find a brick wall or poster hoarding mockup from Unblast)
- These double as social media content — they show you understand outdoor advertising

---

### Phase 5 — Digital and Social (The Hireable Work)

**Step 1: E-commerce app UI (Figma)**
Design 4 mobile screens:
- **Home/Landing:** Hero image with a "Shop Now" or "Explore" button, category navigation at the bottom
- **Product listing:** A clean grid of products — image, name, price. Allow for filtering by category
- **Product detail:** One large product image, size selector, care information, "Add to Bag" button
- **Cart/Checkout:** Order summary, delivery options, payment

Figma rules:
- Use Auto-Layout for every element so the design is properly responsive
- Create a component for the navigation bar, product card, and button — used consistently across all screens
- Prototype at least 3 interactions (tapping a product card goes to the product detail page)

**Step 2: Social media toolkit**
Design 5 Instagram grid post templates and 5 Instagram story templates:
- Grid posts: new drop announcement, product feature, editorial image with text overlay, quote/brand value post, campaign poster format
- Story templates: "New In" announcement, product detail close-up, poll template, countdown timer
- All templates must be "drag and drop" — a placeholder image and an editable text field — so they feel like a systematic, reusable asset, not one-off designs

---

### Phase 6 — Formatting and Presentation (The Final Polish)

**Step 1: Apply to premium mockups**
Rules:
- Every logo must be shown on at least 3 physical applications (labels, packaging, signage, clothing)
- Every poster must be shown on a wall — never as a flat image in isolation
- Every app screen must be shown inside the latest iPhone frame
- Every printed piece (business card, letterhead, line sheet) must be shown as a physical object, not a flat PDF export

Sources for free and paid premium mockups:
- **Mockup Maison** — high-end, editorial quality, free and paid options
- **Artboard Studio** — browser-based mockup tool, no Photoshop required
- **Unblast** — huge free library, very photorealistic
- **Smartmockups** — quick drag-and-drop, good for basic applications

**Step 2: Structure the case study**
For every project on your website, write:
- **The Brief (2–3 sentences):** What was the problem or the prompt?
- **The Challenge (2–3 sentences):** What made this difficult, interesting, or specific?
- **The Solution (2–3 sentences):** What design decisions did you make and why?
- Then follow with images in this order: moodboard / research → process / sketches → final outcomes → mockups in context

**Step 3: Portfolio PDF**
Always have a PDF version ready to email:
- Landscape orientation (screens are horizontal)
- Under 10MB (compress images — recruiters will not wait for a large file to download)
- Curated — only your best 4–6 projects, not everything
- Open with your strongest project — first impressions are permanent
- End with your contact page and website URL

**Step 4: Personal brand consistency**
Before you build anything, define your own:
- 1–2 typefaces for your personal brand
- 1–2 colours for your personal brand
- A minimal logo or wordmark for your name

Your CV, your website, your LinkedIn banner, your PDF portfolio, and your email signature should all use the same visual language. When a hiring manager sees this, they do not see a student. They see a professional who understands brand consistency from the inside out.
