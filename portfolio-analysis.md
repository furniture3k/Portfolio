## 2. What Appears in Every Single Portfolio (6/6)

These are non-negotiable inclusions. If all 6 completely different designers include these, they are industry standard.

### A. About / Introduction Page
Every single portfolio opens with or includes a dedicated about section. Content always includes:
- Name + profession title
- Photo or illustrated portrait
- Short written bio or intro paragraph
- Contact details (email, socials, location)
- Education (institution, qualification, dates)
- Work experience / clients
- Skills (technical and soft)
- Languages spoken

**For your website:** This is your homepage hero or a dedicated `/about` page. It should be the second thing a visitor sees after your work. The photo is essential — all 6 include one.

### B. Final Outcome / Mockup Images
Without exception, every project ends with high-quality images of the final deliverable — whether that is finished garments, packaged products, printed materials, rendered 3D, or app screens. These are always the most polished, staged, or photographed images in the project.

**For your website:** Every project card/thumbnail on your homepage should use an outcome image, not a process image. Outcome images are what hook visitors. Process comes after.

### C. Concept / Moodboard Section
All 6 portfolios begin each project with a conceptual framing — a moodboard, a written brief, or both. Even the shortest portfolio (Yulia, 8 pages) opens with concept and writeup before showing any designs.

**For your website:** Each project should open with a brief conceptual statement — one or two sentences on what the project is about and why. This goes at the top of every project page.

### D. Project Title + Date + Context
Every project is clearly labelled with at minimum: name of project, year completed, and a one-line brief or client description.

**For your website:** Each project card on your homepage needs at least three pieces of metadata: project name, year, and discipline/type.

## 3. What Appears in 5 of 6 Portfolios

These are strongly recommended inclusions. Only missing in one portfolio, usually because of discipline difference.

### A. Process / Development Documentation
5/6 portfolios show in-between stages — not just start and finish. This includes:
- Sketches and exploratory drawings
- Concept iterations and rejected ideas  
- Research pages (style research, fabric research, typography choices)
- Colour and material exploration

**Missing from:** Yulia (pure outcome showcase, no process shown)
**For your website:** Including process makes your work feel thoughtful and intentional. At least 2-4 process images per project. They do not need to be as polished as final outcomes — raw sketches and flat lays are fine and create contrast.

### B. Written Descriptions on Project Pages
5/6 portfolios include written content alongside images. Writeups cover: the brief, the concept rationale, the decisions made, and sometimes the impact.

**Missing from:** Yulia (purely visual)
**For your website:** Short paragraph blocks (50-120 words) alongside key images. Not walls of text — just enough to give images context and show your thinking.

### C. Table of Contents
5/6 portfolios (all except Yulia's short one) have a structured contents page or numbered project listing.

**For your website:** Your homepage acts as your table of contents. Each project should be numbered (01, 02, 03…) as seen in Rhyzza's portfolio — this is a widespread convention that gives work a curated, considered feel.

### D. Colour Swatches / Palette Documentation
5/6 portfolios include dedicated colour palette documentation for each project — swatches, hex references, or fabric colour samples.

**Missing from:** Yulia
**For your website:** Each project page should show a small colour palette strip. This is particularly important for branding and fashion projects. It is one of the quickest ways to communicate the visual language of a project.

## 4. What Appears in 4 of 6 Portfolios

Common but not universal. Worth including if relevant to your discipline.

### A. Social Media Post Mockups (4/6)
Portfolios: Sebastian, Rhyzza, Garry, Mu Studio
All four graphic/branding portfolios include designed social media posts as deliverables. Typically 4-6 posts shown in a grid or staged mockup.

**For your website:** If any of your projects involve branding or campaigns, show social media deliverables. They are immediately relatable to clients and communicate real-world application.

### B. Logo / Logofolio Section (4/6)
Portfolios: Sebastian, Garry, Mu Studio, Rhyzza (brand redesign)
A dedicated section showing logo design work — either as a standalone logofolio or as part of a brand identity case study.

**For your website:** If you have logo work, a logofolio is a high-impact, low-effort section. Even 6-10 logo marks displayed cleanly on a neutral background communicates range and skill.

### C. Typography Documentation (4/6)
Portfolios: Sebastian, Garry, Mu Studio, Lara
Showing typefaces used in a project is standard in design portfolios. Usually shown as font name + specimen/sample text.

**For your website:** For branding projects, a small "fonts used" section on each project page is easy to add and rounds out the brand identity documentation.

### D. Technical Drawings (4/6 — discipline-dependent)
Portfolios: Lara (extensively), Yulia, Sebastian (UX wireframes), Rhyzza (UX wireframes)
Fashion designers use garment technical drawings (flats). UX/UI designers use wireframes and screen flows. These serve the same function: showing structural thinking behind the visual.

**For your website:** Relevant only if you are showing fashion design or UX work. If so, include at least one flat/technical spread per major project — it demonstrates professional competence to industry viewers.

## 5. What Appears in 2-3 of 6 Portfolios

Worth considering as differentiators.

### A. 3D Renders / AI Renderings (3/6)
Portfolios: Yulia, Lara, Mu Studio (implied via mockups)
Yulia's entire portfolio is 3D renders. Lara includes AI renderings as a supporting section. 3D and AI-generated visualisation are increasingly common.

**For your website:** If you produce 3D or AI renderings as part of your process, include them. Label them clearly. Clients and recruiters are not put off by AI-assisted renders — they demonstrate technical range.

### B. VIP / Target Customer Profile (2/6)
Portfolios: Lara ("VIP person that would wear it"), Sebastian (target audience for each project)
Describing who the end user or customer is adds strategic depth to a project.

**For your website:** Even a one-line "designed for X" adds context and shows you think beyond aesthetics. Especially useful for fashion and branding projects.

### C. QR Code to CV / Resume (2/6)
Portfolios: Garry, Sebastian (implied via contact page)
A QR code linking to a downloadable CV is a nice physical-to-digital bridge — more relevant for PDF portfolios but can work on a website as a download button.

**For your website:** A clear "Download CV" button on your about page is standard and expected. Not a QR code — just a button.

### D. Pattern / Print Design (2/6)
Portfolios: Garry, Lara (motif development)
Surface pattern, print design, and repeat pattern work appear as a subsection within larger projects.

**For your website:** If you have pattern/print work, even showing it as a subsection within a broader fashion or branding project adds depth.

## 6. Project Structure — The Universal Template

Across all 6 portfolios, every project follows a near-identical information architecture, regardless of discipline. Map this directly to your website's project page layout:

```
Project Page Structure (Top to Bottom)

1. PROJECT TITLE
   — Large, typographic, full-width or split layout

2. PROJECT META
   — Year | Discipline | Client or brief type

3. BRIEF / CONCEPT STATEMENT
   — 1-3 sentences: what was the problem or prompt

4. MOODBOARD / REFERENCE IMAGES
   — Visual context for the direction

5. PROCESS / DEVELOPMENT
   — Sketches, research, iterations, material/colour choices

6. COLOUR PALETTE
   — Swatches or palette strip

7. TYPOGRAPHY (for design projects)
   — Typefaces used

8. FINAL OUTCOMES / HERO IMAGES
   — Best quality images last, as a reveal

9. MOCKUPS (for branding/graphic design)
   — Real-world application images

10. OPTIONAL: IMPACT / TAKEAWAY
    — What was learned or what the outcome achieved
```

## 9. What Would Make the Strongest Website Projects

Regardless of discipline, the highest-impact project types for a website (as opposed to a PDF) are:

### Tier 1 — Maximum Impact on a Website
- **Brand Identity Case Study** — Multiple deliverables, rich in visual variety (logo, colour, type, mockups). Scrolls beautifully. Garry's wellness brand and Mu Studio's Loop & Line are ideal models.
- **Campaign Design** — Posters, social media, merch. Rhyzza's IPV campaign is a strong example. Cause-driven work communicates values.

### Tier 2 — Strong, Standard Inclusions
- **Fashion Collection** — If your discipline is fashion, a full collection spread (illustrations + tech drawings + outcomes) is essential. Lara's Project 1 is the gold standard example.
- **UX/UI App Design** — Screen recordings or scroll-through interactions work especially well on a website in a way they can't in PDF.

### Tier 3 — Supplementary / Supporting
- **Logofolio** — A grid of 8-12 logos. Strong filler between larger case studies.
- **Packaging** — Works well but needs strong photography/mockup quality to shine online.
- **Print / Editorial** — Mockup photography is critical. Flat scans look weak on screen.

## 10. What to Avoid / What Does NOT Translate from PDF to Website

All 6 portfolios are PDFs or PDF-adjacent Behance presentations. Some things that work in PDF do not work well on a website:

- **Splash/title screens for each project** — You noted this already. The website IS the navigation. Individual project splash pages slow the user down.
- **Multi-page fabric/mood spreads as static images** — Better to make these into a scrollable section or a lightbox gallery rather than a single dense image.
- **Long text blocks** — PDFs tolerate more text. On screen, keep paragraphs under 120 words and use generous spacing.
- **Portrait-oriented layouts** — PDFs are portrait. Websites are landscape/wide. Redesign any portrait-heavy content into horizontal scroll sections or tall vertical scroll sections.
- **Table of contents pages** — Replaced entirely by your site navigation and project index.
- **"Thank you" end pages** — Replaced by your contact section.

## 12. Summary Recommendations

1. **Lead with outcome images everywhere** — thumbnails, hero sections, project cards. Process comes after the hook.
2. **Number your projects** — 01, 02, 03. Signals curation and intentionality.
3. **Every project needs a one-paragraph brief** — even if it is your own self-directed work, frame it as a brief.
4. **Moodboard as entry to every project** — sets the tone before showing work.
5. **Always show colour palettes** — it is the single most-repeated design element across all 6 portfolios.
6. **About page is non-negotiable** — photo, bio, contact, education, experience.
7. **Download CV button on about page** — simple, expected, professional.
8. **3-5 projects is the sweet spot** — more than 7 dilutes focus; fewer than 3 feels underdeveloped.
9. **Mockups for branding work** — never show a logo flat on a white background as a final deliverable. Show it on a tote bag, a screen, a business card.
10. **For fashion work: technical drawings + outcome photography** are both required — one without the other reads as incomplete.
